july 20, 2026

Yesterday I finished two books, which I hadn't realized I was reading in parallel until Exocapitalism pointed to the shadow in the sky and The Dispossesed held my beating heart. What a succession of books! Two summers ago it was Gender Trouble and Orland, also a beautiful accident. It's lovely to be surprised by how things change, how the seed grows.

Poliks & Trillo's Exocapitalism is built on the mechanisms of lift, fold, and drag. It describes an indifferent and independent mechanism for which the human is the "last mile of bones and blood" and all else is arbitrage. The Marxist relations of labor, production, and appropriation is supplanted with a system that is completely indifferent of labor and material, a "lifted" economy of derivatives contracts or rather, the dealing of the possibility of fluctuations in and of itself. I hope I got that right.

Le Guin's The Dispossed places us in a future of many worlds in which Earth is ravaged, capitalism persists on a green planet, and anarchism charts its path, 170 years young, on a desert moon. Our hero is an anarchist, more liberated as well as ambitious in spirit and mind than his trenchant brothers and sisters, and a scientist who, bearing groundbreaking knowledge which his people do not care for, moves to share it (along with the anarchist spirit) with all and not just one empire. In these worlds, capitalism is a funciton of empire and empire is the master hand. It is physically lifted off of the planetary surface but operates in materially - appropriating human labor, imprisoning and shooting the revolutionary proletariat, and so on. It is a visionary masterpiece from 1974 and a deeply moving account of the next thousands of years "of bones and blood."

Is that all? What is the human to the lifted economy? Its authors suggest "drag," which is not which pulls it back but creates new surface for value generation. Humanity recedes to minor details - in which they also posit there is a sort of freedom, that capitalism is already beyond us and the state as well. What then, as materially substantiated beings? How do we reconcile the lift and the ground? Anarchism to Le Guin is a cooperative society grounded in its bare survival. It is also the moon. Pre-revolution planetary society continues in the contradictions, inequities, and violence of empire-wielded capitalism. But if capitalism is also indifferent to empire, which drags across the earth attempting to sustain itself by enabling lift and leaving trenches in its wake, then we are left behind with it. Come from it. Will return to it.

What about those who are, alongside exocapitalism, lifted? Are they too reduced to the scale of human and if so at which timescale? As a child of capitalism, I cannot help but feel desperate. But if the material basis of microchips and data centers are truly irrelevant to exocapitalism - and it is only empire, constituted as states, which seek desperately to maintain some relationship to it - then perhaps it is just empire afterall. Empire which we cannot hope will recognize its folly but almost certainly, destroy us all in the process.

Other worlds. Can we practice other worlds? As if they exist. They must for our hope to catch. And of course, they do. But it will be alas a human story and there is no salvation from ourselves in exocapitalism nor new empires nor safety or security in new systems. It is something unsatisfying like, we hold liberation and imprisonment within ourselves. Surivival might sharpen that picture.

I started Judith Butler's Gender Trouble in the summer of 2024 and applied myself to it for the better part of one year. Like an amateur athlete re-discovering their sport, I sat at the library each Friday parsing the dense academic vocabulary, convoluted sentence structure and philoso-psychoanalytical references. It was immensely pleasurable - at many moments, it took my breath away - and also a bit silly: I hadn't read any of her references (Lacan, Foucault, Kant, Wittig... save Kristeva who I randomly discovered and deployed for a high school paper on The Scarlet Letter) and still insisted I would derrive the meaning of the text. In some ways, I felt I succeeded. In others, I'm certain I extracted only my best guesses.

I can't remember when I learned about Foucault's bio-politics, only that it was years after undergrad and perhaps at Offline or in St. George's bookstore. It sounded compelling and I kept the desire archived until, overcome with appetite and ambition on a train to the Sandberg Fine Arts grad show, I ordered a Foucault reader. It arrived in a flurry of many other such purchases and I almost forgot about it in a stack on my bookshelf. I picked it up yesterday and I've spent yesterday evening and this morning laughing at myself. Reading can be this easy!

Dear Miss Butler, you are so dear to me but why. Is langauge the oldest form of torture and delight? (Apparently our languistic capacity / conscious state is a product of becoming bipedal. See: Damato, 2026:9 citing Stiegler, 2023:187) It seems I'll have to have another taste because Foucault's 'Right of Death and Power over Life' seems to me to articulate everything I understood from Gender Trouble, save the commentary on the French feminists. Could it be that Gender Trouble is actually just an exercise in applying Foucault to the French school? I suppose I need to read Lacan to find out. In any case, I get the hype around Foucault now.

Managerialism: to qualify, measure, appraise, invest, hierarchize, and incorporate (regulate, distribute, and integrate) life into systems of efficient and economic controls. The technology of sexuality (as the object and target of political and biological power) realized in and through regulatory (medical, administrative, juridical) aparatuses.

Once, sitting at the Zimt & Mehl tables by the canal, my beloved friend, Y, said, "if I could only know myself in the ways I am given by the world, I wouldn't know myself at all." Managerialism: to grant access to, relinquish, disavow the right to ensure, maintain, or develop one's life according to the discovery of all that one is and can be.

How do I express how deeply these words affect me? Both my friend's and Foucault/Butler's. I feel it touches a place where everything (sensation, emotion, mind, and sociality) unravels and opens up for me. I understand for myself now that reading and theory can be as life-giving and liberatory as poetry. That I might explore and deepen into the question of how to live through study.

Perhaps this was something my fellow students and post-study friends have always felt and known. Perhaps it's something they lost connection to but feel a return to upon the recovery of the feeling. Or perhaps some also feel that study was too soon, too often, too much a source of alientation and oppression (other side: power and control) to have a wholesome relationship to it. I am grateful for my own macro and micro turns from worship to dismissal to curiosity and desire. I personally feel there remains much to be inspected of these forms and my appetite for them. It is, for me, like other modes of engagement, a practice of intimacy with the self and the overlappings, interactions, and echoes of the world within and around it.
