Yesterday I finished two books, which I hadn't realized I was reading in parallel until Exocapitalism pointed to the shadowy mass overhead and The Dispossesed held my beating heart. What a succession of books! Two summers ago it was Gender Trouble and Orlando, pure serendipity. It's lovely to be surprised by another such rich pairing and in it to notice how things change, how and where the seeds grow.

Poliks & Trillo's Exocapitalism is explains the mechanisms of lift, fold, and drag by which we might better understand capitalism as an indifferent and independent mechanism to which humans are the "last mile of bones and blood." Everything, in exocapitalism, arbitrage. The Marxist relations of labor, production, and appropriation are supplanted by a system that is completely indifferent of labor and material, a "lifted" economy of derivatives contracts hedging on pure fluctuations. Supply/demand is ancient history, please forget about goods and their use value and even their market value. The marketplace is gone and the dark pool of pure abstract capital floats farther and father beyond us. I hope I got that right.

Le Guin's The Dispossed places us in a future of many worlds in which Earth is ravaged, capitalism persists on a distant green planet, and anarchism charts its path, 170 years young, on a desert moon. Our hero is an anarchist, so liberated in spirit and mind as to be suspected, unwelcome by his fellow supposedly-anarchic brothers and sisters. He is a scientist who, bearing groundbreaking knowledge of temporal phsyics but no audience nor peers to share it with, moves to share it (along with the anarchist spirit) beyond the heretofore positively regarded walls. In these worlds, capitalism is a funciton of empire and empire is the master hand. It is physically lifted off of the planetary surface but operates in materially - appropriating human labor, imprisoning and shooting the revolutionary proletariat, and so on. It is a visionary masterpiece from 1974 and a deeply moving account of the next thousands of years "of bones and blood."

Is that all? What is the human to the lifted economy? Exocapitalism's authors suggest "drag," which is not which pulls it back but creates new surface for value generation. Humanity recedes to pure noise - in which they seem to also suggest a sort of freedom, that capitalism is already beyond us and the state as well. What then, as materially substantiated beings? How do we reconcile the lift and the ground? Anarchism to Le Guin is a cooperative society grounded in its bare survival. It is also the moon. Planetary society continues in the contradictions, inequities, and violence of empire-wielded capitalism. But if capitalism is also indifferent to empire, which drags trenches across the earth attempting to sell off its own goods, then we are left behind with it. Come from it. Will return to it.

What about those who are, alongside exocapitalism, lifted? Are they too reduced to the scale of human and if so at which timescale? As a child of capitalism, I could not help but feel, for some time yesterday, desperate. But if the material basis of microchips and data centers are truly irrelevant to exocapitalism - and it is only empire, constituted as states, which seek desperately to maintain some relationship to it - then perhaps it is just empire afterall. Empire which we cannot hope will recognize its folly but almost certainly, destroy us all in the process.

Can we practice other worlds as if they exist? The last part is critical. They must be real for our hope to catch. And of course, they do though more blood 'n bones are in the mix. I listened to an early disintegrator episode while walking frantically towards the company budget hotel in Paris. Just about none of it made sense to me - and made me seriously doubt whether I had any business in writing anything suggesting I have any functional intellect - but he did articulate two things I'm taking away: that people have been throwing Marx out the door since the 90s (specifically in France, specifically Deleuze, Guttari, e t c?) and the thing that's hard to navigate while keeping Marx useful is the shift from industrial to financial capital. Also known as the difference between commodities and assets. Okay. So I've been a little proud of myself for reading this stuff, granted I'm making a lot of guesses at meaning. I'll add generative ones, at least for me as a random entity. But the point is I've been cutting corners, carving crude angles, and taking other people's words at face value.

So I gotta do some more active thinking. It is decently hard when you don't play piano and you want to learn Fantaisie Impromptu (sigh, all these patterns) and I suppose what I'm feeling is I guess I won't become a world-class philosopher (somehow the new-to-me-ness of it all made it feel easier than things I already broke some ground in). Or maybe I will! Hell, I love my fantaisie impromptu these days, she in her fragements is so generative and brings me to my very core. Side note: I feel like I've really found my sound people in the Scissortail crew. So to hell with Hegel, Humes, Kant, Habermas, and all the other men I've never read and might never read. I will read what I can, enjoy it as much as possible, and also, this feels important right now, struggle (or rather, make them struggle) a little harder.

And we made it all the way back to Le Guin! Her protagonist basically says, struggle harder, you'll like it. Safety and security are not life-giving wells: I think we know that by now (look up: exocapitalism is lifting higher). So next systems can't and won't be built off of that. But some daring, delusion, and devilishness would get us (no, not you, financial asset propertarian chosen servant of exocapitalism) going. Surivival will sharpen that picture.

A little bonus story: I was trying to figure out why this unexpected conversation with a self-described ex-military guy from Everett Massachusetts on the Nomad509 bus from Etretat to Le Havre struck me so much today. He runs a defense contracting start-up and we had such a genuine, open-minded and open-hearted chat. It was a delight! And now I know what it was: his devilishness. Again, I don't mean being a literal force of evil (the US empire was put in its place and to his credit, by both of us or honestly, maybe there's more credit due here. He could probably tear it apart in even finer pieces than I could.) but just doing things and however he wants or likes but with real intention and active intellect. Taking up knowledge and playing with it (80 hours a week... maybe training thousands of soldiers in Hawaii*, churning human meat mills at Amazon**, and oh right, human meat mills the joint venture of Amazon with his current work: military profiteering), and making a force of it. I hope he'll join us when it's time. But anyway, novel, no?

*I learned that they have a toxic masculinity class in military training. And I'm a bit embarassed that I actually though we might still not have one? Aside: all of my knowledge about the military is abstract and 0% relational. **He left the military exactly 95 days after his 5 year mandatory service term ended so he would leave with the benefits of having been a fully time(?) soldier. He left Amazon as soon as they dropped the fixed-term bonus/ shares/ x financial asset in his bank account. So, hey that's a player and he's got whatever this economic system is paying him - or at least getting paid off well for generating surplus value / folding in new soldiers? Apparently I too have something for winners. Cancel me.

I started Judith Butler's Gender Trouble in the summer of 2024 and applied myself to it for the better part of one year. Like an amateur athlete re-discovering their sport, I sat at the library each Friday parsing the dense academic vocabulary, convoluted sentence structure and philoso-psychoanalytical references. It was immensely pleasurable - at many moments, it took my breath away - and also a bit silly: I hadn't read any of her references (Lacan, Foucault, Kant, Wittig... save Kristeva who I randomly discovered and deployed for a high school paper on The Scarlet Letter) and still insisted I would derrive the meaning of the text. In some ways, I felt I succeeded. In others, I'm certain I extracted only my best guesses.

I can't remember when I learned about Foucault's bio-politics, only that it was years after undergrad and perhaps at Offline or in St. George's bookstore. It sounded compelling and I kept the desire archived until, overcome with appetite and ambition on a train to the Sandberg Fine Arts grad show, I ordered a Foucault reader. It arrived in a flurry of many other such purchases and I almost forgot about it in a stack on my bookshelf. I picked it up yesterday and I've spent yesterday evening and this morning laughing at myself. Reading can be this easy!

Dear Miss Butler, you are so dear to me but why. Is langauge the oldest form of torture and delight? (Apparently our languistic capacity / conscious state is a product of becoming bipedal. See: Damato, 2026:9 citing Stiegler, 2023:187) It seems I'll have to have another taste because Foucault's 'Right of Death and Power over Life' seems to me to articulate everything I understood from Gender Trouble, save the commentary on the French feminists. Could it be that Gender Trouble is actually just an exercise in applying Foucault to the French school? I suppose I need to read Lacan to find out. In any case, I get the hype around Foucault now.

Managerialism: to qualify, measure, appraise, invest, hierarchize, and incorporate (regulate, distribute, and integrate) life into systems of efficient and economic controls. The technology of sexuality (as the object and target of political and biological power) realized in and through regulatory (medical, administrative, juridical) aparatuses.

Once, sitting at the Zimt & Mehl tables by the canal, my beloved friend, Y, said, "if I could only know myself in the ways I am given by the world, I wouldn't know myself at all." Managerialism: to grant access to, relinquish, disavow the right to ensure, maintain, or develop one's life according to the discovery of all that one is and can be.

How do I express how deeply these words affect me? Both my friend's and Foucault/Butler's. I feel it touches a place where everything (sensation, emotion, mind, and sociality) unravels and opens up for me. I understand for myself now that reading and theory can be as life-giving and liberatory as poetry. That I might explore and deepen into the question of how to live through study.

Perhaps this was something my fellow students and post-study friends have always felt and known. Perhaps it's something they lost connection to but feel a return to upon the recovery of the feeling. Or perhaps some also feel that study was too soon, too often, too much a source of alientation and oppression (other side: power and control) to have a wholesome relationship to it. I am grateful for my own macro and micro turns from worship to dismissal to curiosity and desire. I personally feel there remains much to be inspected of these forms and my appetite for them. It is, for me, like other modes of engagement, a practice of intimacy with the self and the overlappings, interactions, and echoes of the world within and around it.
